# What do you make of this? I’m watching a video of The James Gang playing Funk #49. There’s a lot of posturing, fabulous costumes, and distortion. I just had a sinking feeling that the distortion — this dirty sound — is standing in for funk and ultimately for blackness.



That's a sharp observation, and it cuts right to the heart of what distortion means culturally, not just acoustically.

  There's a real pattern there. The James Gang were a white band from Cleveland who named a song "Funk #49" — the word "funk" itself is
   doing cultural work, pointing toward Black musical traditions (James Brown, Sly Stone, Parliament) while the band delivers it
  through a rock power trio format. The distortion becomes a kind of sonic blackface — it signifies rawness, earthiness, sexuality,
  "the body" — all things that white American culture has historically projected onto Blackness. The "dirty" sound lets the performers
  access those associations without having to name them.

  It's worth noting the asymmetry: when Black musicians like Hendrix used distortion, it was often received as dangerous or
  threatening. When white bands adopted the same sonic vocabulary (plus the costumes, the posturing), it became "classic rock" — safely
   rebellious, commercially enormous.

  The sinking feeling makes sense. Distortion in that context isn't just a clipping waveform — it's a shortcut to an affect that's
  borrowed from a specific cultural lineage. The "dirt" is doing racial work.

  This seems like it connects directly to your course material. The physics of distortion are one thing — harmonics, clipping
  thresholds, transfer functions — but the semiotics of distortion are tangled up in this longer history of who gets to sound "raw" and
   what that rawness is understood to represent.

Technology itself is racialized here too: the gear, signal chains, and performance contexts get coded as a **safe**, white way to traffic in sounds historically linked to Blackness.[1][2][3][4]

## Technologies that make Blackness “usable”

- Weheliye talks about “sonic technologies of Blackness,” where devices like the phonograph, microphone, and studio become ways to capture, circulate, and recontextualize Black sound as raw material.[2][5][6][1]
- Once that sound is detachable from Black bodies, it can be routed through white bodies, white genres, and white-owned infrastructures without looking like “race” anymore—just “good tone,” “authentic distortion,” “classic gear.”[5][6][7][1]

## How whiteness makes tech feel “neutral” and “safe”

- Philip Ewell’s “white racial frame” work in music theory is useful as an analogy: whiteness presents its own structures as neutral, professional, and universal, while treating Blackness as particular, risky, or supplementary.[8][9][10][11]
- You can map that onto tech: Marshall stacks, big concert PAs, multitrack studios, labels, FM rock radio, and later classic-rock reissue culture all position distorted rock guitar as the default, safe way to be loud and “dirty.”[3][4][1][5]
- The same basic signal path used by a Black band is more likely to be heard as dangerous, deviant, or niche; plugged into white rock, it’s heritage, tradition, a dad-amp.[12][13][14][3]

## Distortion rigs as racialized infrastructure

- Waksman effectively shows that specific circuits and devices—the Fender and Gibson electrics, overdriven amps, pedals—become associated with white, masculinist rock virtuosity, even though they emerge from racially mixed and Black idioms.[3]
- The “safety” you’re sensing in the James Gang clip is partly that the whole technological surround (venue, amplification, recording, distribution) is calibrated to keep this dirt within a white, marketable frame.[4][13][12][3]
- So the distortion is not just a timbral choice; it’s a node in a racialized network of tech, labor, and genre that lets white performers borrow the charge of Blackness while being buffered by white institutions.[1][4][12][3]

## Thinking this through in a classroom / studio

- One way to foreground the “whiteness” of the technology would be to diagram the full signal chain (instrument → amp → stage → room → recording → circulation) and mark where ownership, control, and risk sit racially at each point.[7][4][1][3]
- Another is to have students compare two visually similar rigs—say, Hendrix vs. a white contemporary using comparable distortion—and then ask not “what does the gear do?” but “what kinds of bodies and stories does this rig make safe or unsafe?”[13][14][12][3]

Would you want to push this into a more explicit “infrastructure of whiteness” angle—e.g., labels, radio formats, and live sound—rather than staying at the level of instruments and pedals?

You’re putting your finger on something a lot of the critical literature is circling: distortion as a racialized *signifier* of “funk,” “rawness,” and “the body,” especially once white rock takes it up as style rather than lived social location.[1][2][3][4]

## Distortion as racialized “dirt”

- In that James Gang clip, the overdrive, swagger, and “funk” branding sit on top of a power‑trio rock grammar that’s already been whitened by late‑60s industry and criticism.[2][5][6][1]
- As Jack Hamilton shows, rock history got written so that rock’s loud, distorted sounds became the authentic property of white “artists,” while parallel Black practices were pushed into separate bins (“soul,” “R&B,” “funk”) or read as less universal.[6][7][1][2]
- That lets distortion function the way you describe: a **shortcut** to an affect coded as Black (dirty, bodily, sensual, dangerous) but “safe” when mediated through white performers and white‑marketed genres.[3][4][1][2]

## Sonic blackness and “authenticity”

- Alexander Weheliye’s idea of “sonic Afro‑modernity” is useful here: Blackness is not just in who plays, but in how sound and technology get heard as Black or not.[8][9]
- Karl Hagstrom Miller tracks how the industry literally segregated sound—assigning certain timbres, grooves, and recording contexts to “race records” vs “hillbilly,” even when musicians were drawing from the same shared pool of practices.[4]
- Your sinking feeling matches that history: the “dirty” electric guitar tone is no longer neutral; it’s sticky with decades of discourses about authenticity, Blackness, and who gets to embody “the real thing.”[10][11][3]

## When Black musicians distort

- The asymmetry you mention shows up in how Hendrix, Funkadelic, or later Black rock/metal artists are framed as excessive, dangerous, or niche, while white guitar heroes using comparable distortion are canonized as classic rock.[1][2][3][10]
- Maureen Mahon’s work on the Black Rock Coalition is partly about what happens when Black musicians insist on claiming distorted guitar music that critics and labels have already decided “belongs” to white rock.[10]
- Daphne Brooks and Weheliye both push toward listening practices that can actually hear Black experimentalism around noise, distortion, and studio artifice as theory and world‑making, not just as raw material to be mined.[9][12][13][8]

## Some concrete reading paths

- Jack Hamilton, *Just around Midnight: Rock and Roll and the Racial Imagination* – on how rock becomes white and how critics racialize sounds and affects.[7][2][6][1]
- Steve Waksman, *Instruments of Desire* – on the electric guitar’s “dirty” sound, gender, and race; how distortion becomes a phallic, racialized performance technology.[11][3]
- Maureen Mahon, *Right to Rock* and her article “African Americans and Rock ’n’ Roll” – on Black rockers, distortion, and reclaiming rock idioms.[10]
- Karl Hagstrom Miller, *Segregating Sound* – on how the industry built race into genre and timbre.[4]
- Alexander Weheliye, *Phonographies* and “Engendering Phonographies” – on sonic technologies of Blackness.[8][9]
- Daphne Brooks, *Liner Notes for the Revolution* – on Black feminist listening and the politics of how we hear popular music.[12][13]

## Assignment overview

Design and build a custom distortion circuit, then create a short audio piece with it and write a critical reflection (1000–1500 words) on how your distortion participates in histories of sonic Blackness, whiteness, error, and “safety.”  

## Part 1 – Circuit and sound

1. **Design / modify a distortion circuit**  
   - Start from: a simple overdrive/fuzz schematic, a bent toy, or an op‑amp clipping circuit.  
   - You must intentionally shape at least two of these: clipping behavior, frequency emphasis (tone shaping), dynamic response (how it reacts to touch), or noise floor.  

2. **Document the build**  
   - Schematic (even hand‑drawn), annotated to show where you introduced “error” (e.g., wrong‑value part, abused power rails, mis‑biasing).[1][2][3]
   - 3–5 photos of the process (breadboard, guts, final enclosure).  

3. **Create a 1–3 minute audio piece**  
   - Perform a short piece that uses your distortion as a central voice.  
   - The source can be guitar, voice, drum machine, found sound, etc.; at least one source must be your own recording.  
   - No “neutral” mix: make at least one bold decision about level, noise, or harshness that most commercial producers would avoid.[3][4][5]

## Part 2 – Critical reflection (1000–1500 words)

Structure the reflection around three moves: description, lineage, politics.

### A. Describe your distortion as a listener

- Describe how your distortion sounds in as much sensory detail as you can (not just “warm” / “fizzy,” but “splatty on transients, smeared sustain, high‑mid screech,” etc.).  
- Describe how it feels to play/listen through it: What does it do to your body? Where does it sit between control and loss of control?  

Prompt:  
- “What parts of my body (hands, lungs, ears, nerves) does this circuit ask the most of, and how?”  

### B. Situate it in lineages

Have students listen to at least two tracks:

- One by a Black artist using guitar or signal distortion (e.g., Hendrix, Parliament/Funkadelic, Prince, Betty Davis, Bad Brains, Death, etc.).[6][7][8][9]
- One by a white artist using distortion in a recognizably rock idiom (e.g., James Gang, Clapton, Zeppelin, Clash, etc.).[10][11][12][6]

Then:

- Compare your distortion’s timbre and feel to those tracks. Who do you sound “next to”? Who do you *not* sound like?  
- Speculate on what genre bins your sound would likely be sorted into by a label or algorithm (rock, metal, experimental, noise, “urban,” etc.).[9][11][13][10]

Prompts:  
- “If a streaming service heard only my distortion tone, which genre or playlist would it probably file me under, and why?”  
- “Which artists would be named as my ‘influences’ in a press release—and what does that say about race, genre, and distortion?”  

### C. Politics of error, safety, and access

Here you push them toward the “hope” question.

Ask them to address:

- **Error as method**: Where did you deliberately or accidentally break engineering rules (wrong bias, unstable feedback, clipping everything)? How did that “error” become the core of your sound?[5][14][3]
- **Safety vs. risk**: Would this sound be legible as “safely rebellious classic rock,” “dangerous noise,” “Black futurist funk,” “DIY art school experimental,” etc.? What kinds of bodies and venues does it seem built for? Bar band, warehouse rave, church, gallery, bedroom?[7][11][6][10]
- **Access and cost**: How much did your circuit cost, and what skills did it require? How does that compare to buying a boutique pedal or a full amp stack? What does that difference mean for who gets to enter into distorted sound at all?[2][4][3]

Explicit prompt set (they must answer all three):  

1. “In what ways does my circuit reproduce existing racialized ideas of what distorted sound ‘should’ be? In what ways, if any, does it push against them?”  
2. “What kinds of spaces and listeners does my distortion seem designed to feel ‘at home’ with—and who might feel out of place inside it?”  
3. “How does the fact that I built this cheaply, from parts and errors, change how I understand the ‘whiteness’ or ‘safety’ of distortion technologies?”  

Encourage them to reference at least one reading on race and sound (Hamilton, Mahon, Weheliye, Waksman, etc.) and at least one text on DIY / circuit‑bending or glitch as pedagogy.[4][15][16][3][5][6]

## Deliverables and evaluation

- Circuit + documentation (schematic, photos).  
- 1–3 minute audio file.  
- 1000–1500 word reflection responding to the prompts above, with at least two citations (one on race/sonic Blackness, one on DIY / error / glitch).[15][16][3][4][5][6]

Grading emphasis:  
- 30% technical engagement (the circuit does something clearly intentional).  
- 30% sonic exploration (they actually use its “wrongness” musically).  
- 40% depth of reflection on race, technology, error, and “who this sound is for,” not just autobiography.
