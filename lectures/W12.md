# WEEK 12: Distortion Circuits

**Fuzz vs Overdrive — Designing Nonlinearity**

---

# Two Philosophies

- **Overdrive:** Simulate amp breakup at low volume — soft clipping, dynamic, cleans up with guitar volume
- **Fuzz:** Deliberately obliterate the signal — hard clipping, sustain, compressed

---

# Overdrive Circuit

- Gain stage with diodes in the feedback loop
- Soft clipping — rounds off peaks
- Signal retains dynamics and picking nuance
- Think: tube amp at moderate volume

---

# Fuzz Circuit

- High-gain transistor stages driving into hard clipping
- Two transistors in series: Q1 boosts, Q2 clips
- Diode from collector to base creates feedback clipping
- Cleanup: roll back guitar volume and the fuzz thins out

---

# The Bazz Fuss

- Simplest useful fuzz: one transistor, one diode, a few passives
- Build it, bias it, starve it
- When biased hot: thick, broken
- When starved (low voltage): gated, sputtery
- The "bias" knob = 50k pot in series with collector resistor

---

# Filters Shape Everything

- Same clipping circuit sounds completely different with pre/post EQ
- Bass before clipping → flubby, loose
- Treble before clipping → harsh, cutting
- Bass cut before + bass boost after → tight and heavy (modern metal trick)

# Listening

- Jimi Hendrix "Foxy Lady" — fuzz with volume knob cleanup
- Rolling Stones "Satisfaction" — Maestro Fuzz-Tone, one of the first commercial units
- Stevie Ray Vaughan — overdrive (Tube Screamer), dynamics intact
- Compare: Hendrix fuzz vs SRV overdrive on the same riff

**What to listen for:** Cleanup behavior. Sustain character. Does the picking hand still matter?

# Paired Listening: Who Gets to Sound "Dirty"?

Listen to two performances that both lean on guitar distortion:

- **Black artist:** Choose from Hendrix, Funkadelic ("Maggot Brain," "Hit It and Quit It"), Betty Davis, Bad Brains, Death (the Detroit band), Prince
- **White artist:** Choose from James Gang ("Funk #49"), Cream, Led Zeppelin, The Clash, Black Sabbath

Watch video if possible — not just audio.

**What to listen for:** Not just the distortion tone, but the whole frame. How are the bodies presented? What's the staging, the costuming? How does the camera relate to the performer? Does the distortion read as dangerous, playful, virtuosic, safe?

**What to ask:** Does the same clipping waveform mean the same thing coming from different bodies, in different genre contexts, marketed to different audiences?

# The Politics of Distortion

The physics of distortion are one thing — harmonics, clipping thresholds, transfer functions. But the *semiotics* of distortion have a racial history.

- **"Dirty" is not neutral.** The language we use for distortion — dirty, raw, filthy, nasty, gritty — maps onto qualities that white American culture has historically projected onto Blackness: earthiness, the body, sexuality, danger.
- **The genre split.** Jack Hamilton (*Just around Midnight*, 2016) traces how in the late 1960s, "rock" became white and "soul/funk/R&B" became Black — even though both traditions shared the same instruments, amplifiers, and distortion circuits. Sonic signifiers, including distortion, were central to that split.
- **Asymmetry of reception.** When Black musicians like Hendrix used distortion, it was received as threatening or excessive. When white bands adopted the same sonic vocabulary, it became "classic rock" — safely rebellious, commercially enormous.
- **Technology is not neutral either.** Steve Waksman (*Instruments of Desire*, 1999) shows how specific circuits and devices — Fender and Gibson electrics, overdriven amps, pedals — became associated with white rock virtuosity, even though they emerged from racially mixed and Black musical traditions.
- **Reclaiming.** Maureen Mahon (*Right to Rock*, 2004) documents what happens when Black musicians insist on claiming distorted guitar music that critics and labels have already decided "belongs" to white rock.

**The question for builders:** When you design a distortion circuit, you're not just choosing a clipping threshold. You're entering a network of associations — genre, race, gender, class — that will shape how your sound is heard. Who is your distortion "for"? What body does it imagine holding the guitar?
