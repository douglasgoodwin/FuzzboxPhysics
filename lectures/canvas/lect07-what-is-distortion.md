# LECT07: What is Distortion?

Today's question is about distortion. What is it? What does it sound like? Why do we like it? How do we describe it? Can we quantify it? What would that quantification look like?

Let's start with some basic qualities. What does distortion feel like to you? What words would you use for it? One thing that came up in class was that distortion can sound **squishy**. It can sound **compressed**. It can make the signal feel **more self-similar**. It can **add texture**. It can make a sound **fuzzy**. Those are good words. Keep them!

Now let's listen to this example of clean guitar versus distorted guitar. Notice that this is an amplified electric guitar. It is actually rather difficult to get much sound at all out of an unamplified electric guitar, so that is already worth thinking about. This instrument depends on electronics. It depends on mediation.

[Video: Clean Guitar Vs Distorted Guitar - Brennan]

The clean signal, we might describe as round, resonant, and less saturated. Even when it is loud, it still seems to contain more silence. The distorted signal, by contrast, sounds full. It sounds compressed. It sounds saturated. It has more texture. It is fuzzy. It has more apparent density.

In audio engineering, distortion refers to any change from an ideal, linear version of a signal. In popular music, though, we often use 'distortion' for the specific, saturated, noisy sounds that came from amplification and recording technologies.

One thing I want you to notice is that some of the earliest guitar sounds people identified as exciting or expressive were sounds that showed signs of stress. That should remind you of the voices we talked about at the beginning of this unit, singers stretching their bodies in ways that created vocal distortion. Something similar happens with guitar sound. We hear effort. We hear strain. We hear a signal pushed toward a limit.

Here's a provocation: The kind of distortion we usually mean in rock and popular music is *mostly produced by recording and amplification*. It is a convention. Even though we associate it with extremity, effort, and force, it is also a mediated sound. It belongs to engineering. It belongs to audio systems. Some of these changes are nonlinear (they create new frequencies--classic 'distortion' in the engineering sense). Others, like simple filtering or reverb, are linear transformations, but for now we'll group them together under 'ways a signal can be altered.'

That is part of why distortion is bound up with performance style. Rock and roll always seems to want to appear as strain, as extreme activity, as forceful sound generated by extreme physical exertion. But think about that for a second. If you were at the top of your game, playing shows every night or every other night for years, you would presumably be very good at playing guitar. You would not literally need to struggle in the way rock musicians often seem to present themselves as struggling.

[Video: Link Wray - Rumble | Live at Winterland (1974)]

It's posturing. It is a convention. It's drag. Distortion is bound up with that. It is bound up with the sound of rock and roll and with the lifestyle attached to it.

Punk Rock even more. At that point it is not necessarily about instrumental mastery at all. It can become a matter of ferocity, attitude, refusal, abrasion. I'm not suggesting that you ignore the posture. Quite the opposite! Attitude will take you a long way. But it is important to understand that what is being performed there is not simply raw effort. It is also style, convention, and mediation.

So when we talk about distortion, and when you describe it as squishing, compression, self-similarity, texture, or fuzziness, you are really describing artifacts in an audio signal. You are describing ways a signal gets changed. That is why the electric guitar is such a perfect instrument for thinking about this. Without the electronics, it is basically silent. Its whole world is magnetic fields, amplification, transduction, and signal processing. The distortion emerges from that whole chain.

That is what I want you to think about in today's lab. I want you to find analog ways of distorting a signal. Your descriptive terms are already strong: squishing, compression, making the signal more self-similar, adding texture, fuzzing. But let's add some other terms too: rattle, buzz, choke.

Some instruments have these mediations built into them physically. A snare drum is a great example. The wires on the bottom buzz when the membrane vibrates. A resonator guitar has a metal cone that vibrates along with the strings. A kalimba sometimes has little metal rings or attachments that create a buzzy edge. A jaw harp is a spring of metal amplified and distorted by the cavity of your mouth. A sizzle cymbal has pieces of metal attached to it to produce a buzzing, ringing texture. A kazoo takes your voice and introduces a membrane that alters it. A cuica gives you another kind of friction-based distortion.

You could also get distortion by damaging a speaker cone. That is part of the old story about Link Wray poking holes in a loudspeaker. Or you could record onto cassette tape and let the tape itself, its hiss, its softness, its magnetic hysteresis, contribute its own distortion to the signal.

And some of you found other examples too, which I was glad to see. You can distort a ringing object just by touching it. If a bell is oscillating and you wrap your hand around it, you are damping that oscillation and changing the sound in a very physical way. That is also a form of analog distortion. It is a mediation of vibration.

What I really want you to develop is an intuition for how the signal is being changed. Eventually I am going to ask you to reproduce some of these effects electronically, so start listening now in a more analytical way. What exactly is happening?

Sometimes a signal is being filtered. Sometimes it is being transposed. Sometimes new frequencies are drawn in because one resonant system is exciting another. Sometimes energy is being transferred. Sometimes a frequency range is being emphasized. Sometimes the attack is being altered. Sometimes the sustain changes. Sometimes noise is added.

There is a whole world of things that can happen to a signal, and many of them can later be reproduced with electronics.

Filtering is one example. A high-pass or low-pass filter changes what parts of the signal are allowed through. Reverb is another example, amplifying or extending certain parts of a signal in time and frequency. Resonant circuits can behave a little like acoustic resonators. Conversion to and from magnetism is another route. Movement is another. Think about the rotating speaker in a Hammond organ cabinet. As it spins, it creates a Doppler-like effect. That motion itself becomes part of the sound.

So these are all possible ways of thinking about distortion: not just as fuzz or overload, but as transformation. Distortion is a way of changing a signal. It can compress it, color it, saturate it, roughen it, choke it, excite it, or redirect it. It can make it feel more alive, more stressed, more mediated, more expressive.

**For today's lab, I want you to start by capturing your own distortion.**

We have been thinking about noise and distortion already. We have listened to bodies creating strain in the throat. We have listened to early guitars and accidental distortion. We have looked at waveforms and spectrograms and tried to describe distortion through those systems. Now it is time to make some.

Start with a clean, undistorted sound: a bell, a piano, a voice, or a recorded sound. Record it. Then describe the correspondence between what you hear and what you see in Audacity.

Next, distort that sound using some analog means. That part is up to you. Try metal, wax paper, a comb, a slide flute, or some other physical intervention. Then record it again with the distortion added.

Compare the two tracks in Audacity. Look and listen carefully. What changed? What became more compressed? What got noisier? What became fuzzier, buzzier, more rattling, or more choked?
