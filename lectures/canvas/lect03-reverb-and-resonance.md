# LECT03 Reverb and Resonance

**LECT03 FUZZBOX PHYSICS**

Does anyone know the difference between resonance and reverb? I will admit up front that I am not entirely sure I can draw the cleanest line between them, so I want to start with your intuition. When you say "reverb," what do you mean you are hearing? When you say "resonance," what do you mean is happening?

Let me try to pin it down by using a single example we are about to hear. In Alvin Lucier's "I Am Sitting in a Room," both things are present, but they show up in different ways. Resonance is about which frequencies get reinforced. Reverb is about how sound lingers in time as many reflections blur together. If you keep that in mind while we listen, the distinction becomes audible rather than theoretical.

Before we go there, I want to widen the frame for a moment. What about an echo? The Wile E. Coyote version: you shout into a canyon and you hear it bounce back. If you want a nearby place that behaves like that, Vasquez Rocks is right down the road. It is basically a natural echo chamber. You scramble up one of those tilted sandstone walls, there is another wall facing it, and you get that repeating call-and-return. And then there is Red Rocks, outside Denver, which is a built amphitheater inside a geological one. People talk about it like it has magic acoustics, and it does have a signature. But it is also the kind of place where, if you are in the wrong seat, you can see a hit on the stage and hear it a beat later. In the back, getting a drink, the delay can be obvious. So these spaces are not neutral. They have a sound. They impose themselves.

That is what I mean when I say spaces have signatures. Some spaces create pockets where certain frequencies feel amplified. Some spaces create unevenness. You can walk a few feet and the balance changes. You get standing waves. You can get whistling. If you are doing sound professionally, you either work with those conditions or you fight them, but either way you are negotiating with the room.

Now, I want to step back and name the shared idea underneath all of these examples. A room, a wineglass, a swing, a violin string, and a bridge all have natural modes of vibration determined by geometry, mass distribution, and constraints. When energy is fed into one of those modes in a sustained, organized way, the motion can grow to a large amplitude because the system does not dissipate that added energy quickly enough. Where the examples differ is in what counts as the driving force, and how tightly it has to match a fixed frequency.

A good way to feel that difference is the violin bow. When you draw a bow across a string, the bow does not "scan" around looking for a frequency. It enforces a specific kind of stick-slip motion. The rosin makes the hair stick to the string for part of each cycle and drag it sideways, and then the string tension pulls it free and it slips back quickly. That stick-slip creates a sharp traveling kink in the string that races to the bridge and nut, reflects, and comes back. If your bow speed, pressure, and contact point are in the right range, the bow grabs again just as that traveling kink returns, and the whole pattern locks into a repeating cycle. The period of that cycle is set by the string's length, tension, and mass per unit length, not by the timing of your arm. And you know what it sounds like when it fails: bow too hard, too soft, too close to the bridge, too far away, and it breaks into squeaks and noise because that stick-slip can no longer synchronize cleanly.

Now I want to show you a piece that makes the room the subject. This is Alvin Lucier's "I Am Sitting in a Room." As we listen, I want you to take notes. Use paper, use your phone, whatever is easiest. I want you to track what you hear changing. Try to notice when speech becomes texture, when texture becomes tone, and what tones start to dominate.

Here is the core idea. In "I Am Sitting in a Room," resonance and reverb gradually strip away the features of Lucier's voice and replace them with the voice of the room itself. Over many generations of playback and rerecording, you end up hearing mostly sustained tones that correspond to the room's resonant frequencies rather than intelligible speech. It is like the architecture slowly takes over the signal.

Mechanically, it is simple. Lucier records himself reading a short text. Then he plays that recording back into the same room and records it again. Then he plays that new recording back into the room and records it again. And he keeps repeating. Each pass sends the sound through the room's acoustics one more time. So the room's reverb and resonances get applied repeatedly. You can think of it as feedback through a filter whose shape is defined by the room.

Now, let me pull the definitions out of that mechanism, in a way you can hear.

Resonance is the frequency-selective part. It is the selective boost or prolonging of specific frequencies in a system. In a room, those are standing-wave modes: certain pitches ring longer than their neighbors because the geometry and boundaries of the space support them. Spectrally, resonance looks like narrow peaks. And in Lucier, you hear those peaks clearly toward the end, when the words are gone and what remains are relatively pure-ish tones. Those are the room modes asserting themselves, because in this iterative loop they get reinforced again and again.

Reverb is the time-domain part. It is the dense cloud of reflections after a sound: every surface throws back delayed copies, and they overlap until they form a continuous decay. It is broadband in the sense that it affects most of what you hear at once, even if some frequencies decay faster than others. Perceptually, reverb is the wash, the halo, the sense of distance and space. And in Lucier, each playback adds another pass through that reverb field, smearing articulation more and more. The crisp consonants lose their edges. The speech becomes a continuous texture.

And here is how the two relate. Resonances are like the internal "modes" inside the overall reverb: they are the specific frequencies the reverb system holds onto especially well. A space with strong resonances has an uneven decay: some bands ring on while others die quickly. That is why you can have a small room that feels boomy at certain frequencies without sounding lush in a concert-hall way, and you can have a large hall with a smooth, beautiful decay that is not dominated by a few obnoxious peaks. In Lucier, you are hearing both: the reverb blurs speech in time, and the resonances select which frequencies survive the longest.

So as we listen, I want you to listen with that in mind. Listen for when reverb starts to erase the articulations. Listen for when resonance starts to "pick winners," when you can point to a pitch and say, that one is hanging on. Listen for the moment where the piece flips from language to music.

Listen for when distinct pitches begin to emerge. Listen for high tones. Listen for low tones. Listen for anything in the middle that seems to track the voice. And listen for timbre. Some of you may hear something metallic. If you do, do not treat that as a poetic metaphor. Treat it as a technical question. Where is that "metal" coming from? Is it reflectivity? Is it the device chain? Is it what happens when narrow frequency bands start dominating?

*Timbre: the tone color or quality of a sound that makes one instrument or voice sound different from another, even on the same note at the same volume.*

Now, I want to connect this to a broader set of cases. A room is multimode: you excite it with broadband content, and it selects and reinforces certain frequencies. A wineglass is sharply tuned: if you sing one steady note near its resonance, you get classic forced resonance, cycle after cycle adding energy in phase until damping or fracture stops it. A swing is easiest to understand in terms of phase and timing: you add energy at the bottom of the arc, or by pumping your legs, and amplitude builds without you needing to think about generating a pure sinusoid. A bowed string sits in between: you are not imposing a frequency so much as setting up a nonlinear interaction that locks into a stable periodic motion the string can support.

*In acoustics, **multimode** sound means sound in which many different room modes (resonances of the space) are being excited at once, rather than just one dominant resonance.*

I want to be precise about the Tacoma Narrows Bridge. The bridge does have natural vibration modes, but the collapse is not best explained as "the wind matched the frequency and resonance destroyed it." What happens there is aeroelastic flutter: airflow and structure become coupled into a feedback loop. The motion of the bridge modifies the flow in a way that feeds energy back into the motion. Once that system enters an unstable regime, small oscillations grow without needing an externally tuned periodic push.

So you can put these on a spectrum. Passive resonance: the room, the glass. Self-organized periodic motion under the right conditions: the bowed string. Active timing: the swing. Coupled feedback between a system and its environment: Tacoma Narrows. And Lucier is interesting because he shows how you can take a passive system, a room, and create a feedback process by iterating playback through it until the room's natural modes dominate the result.

That is the conceptual frame. Now here is what we are going to do with it. We are going to treat Lucier's piece not as history, but as a method. In pairs or groups of three, you are going to go to different rooms and try your own version of the process. Two phones is enough: one to play back, one to record. Or any record-and-playback devices you have. Your job is to find out what the room does. How quickly does speech become texture? How quickly does texture become tone? What pitches emerge? What stays stable if you change your position? What changes if you change devices?
