# LECT06

## The First CalArts Credo

**No information in advance of need**

**No tool before need**

**No technique before content**

Paul Brach, the founding Dean of Art School, described it this way: instead of front-loading techniques "based on 'you never know when you'll need this,' they started with the idea of 'no information in advance of need.'" This is what people are usually referencing with the shorthand CalArts "credo" you're quoting.

Just-in-time compiling, JIT, was popularized by the Java world, and it has found its way into all kinds of places, including alternative Python runtimes and libraries. Really, most high-level languages today, where you are not talking to each bit individually, depend on specialized libraries that implement performance-critical operations. Fourier transforms are a good example. If you are trying to analyze frequencies in sound, there are well-developed mathematical methods for doing that, and in the early nineteenth century Joseph Fourier showed that complicated functions can be decomposed into sums of simple sine and cosine waves. That is the basis for modern frequency analysis. There is actual, specific math for doing this kind of thing.

I think this is a good example because it is pretty heavy math, and I would never expect anyone in here to master it. Maybe conceptually, if you really wanted to, it is interesting. It also matters because we make all kinds of claims about perception that depend on this model. We say that the ear, in effect, performs a kind of frequency analysis physically, in the body, using the cochlea.

The cochlea is named after a snail shell because it resembles one: a fluid-filled spiral where sound-driven vibrations travel along a membrane. The coiling helps it fit inside the skull, and most of the frequency-selective work comes from how the mechanical properties of the membrane change along its length. Inside, the basilar membrane is narrow and stiff at one end and wider and more flexible at the other, and it is lined with tiny hair cells whose stereocilia bend when sound-driven waves move the membrane and fluid. A given pure tone, like middle C, produces a traveling wave that peaks at a particular place along this membrane, and the hair cells at that location respond most strongly. In a healthy ear, this place-frequency relationship, the cochlea's tonotopic map, is very consistent.

That may be why, when you yawn, tones sometimes seem to shift in pitch. Some of you have had that experience. It is weird, right? I do not know it for a fact, but I suspect it has more to do with pressure changes and muscle activity in the middle ear temporarily altering how sound is transmitted than with literally stretching the cochlea itself. Whatever the exact mechanism, small physical changes in the hearing system can change the pattern of activity reaching the brain for a given tone. You have this long structure where different areas respond best to different frequencies, harmonics create their own spatial and temporal patterns, and your brain turns all of that into the tones you hear. It is a remarkable trick.

Imagine building an artificial version of that, a system that maps where different frequencies land, and your brain does a remarkably good job of interpreting the result. My father was a musician, and he had perfect pitch. Later in life, after severe liver disease and a near-fatal medical crisis, his pitch perception shifted by about a note and a half. He had to remap all of music. He would sit down at the piano and everything sounded wrong. He eventually retrained himself, but it bothered him for the last decade of his life.

There is evidence that pitch perception depends on distributed networks in auditory cortex and connected frontal and parietal systems, and that injury to those networks can produce specific deficits in musical pitch processing without causing global deafness.

So I do not think of what happened to him as a vague psychological disturbance, or as a tiny mechanical problem in the ear. The better explanation is that the trauma altered the neural map that linked heard pitch to stable note categories. In other words, the internal reference shifted. That would explain both why everything suddenly sounded wrong and why he could, with effort, retrain himself: the system was still plastic, but it was now recalibrating from a damaged or misaligned baseline.

That is a remarkable and sobering thing to think about. Music can feel permanent, like a fixed external truth, but some part of it is also a deeply embodied agreement between the world, the ear, and the brain. Change the system, and the world itself seems to move.

These systems are amazing, and we take them for granted because what else do we have. I am not necessarily saying you need to appreciate yourself more (but why not?) we are full of amazing things. The point here is that we already have all these systems inside us that we can tap into, use, and play against. In some idealized world, maybe that would be enough. Maybe you could just learn what you need as you go and not worry too much about technique in advance.

That actually connects to that old CalArts pedagogical mantra: no information in advance of need. Sometimes people paraphrase it as no tool before need, or no technique before content. I like that principle, and I take it seriously. I do not think the answer is to front-load a bunch of abstract technique on the chance that maybe someday you might use it. But once you want to shape sound intentionally, once you want to know why one circuit cuts treble and another cuts bass, once you want to build a pedal rather than just stumble into one by accident, the need has arrived. So today is not technique before content. It is technique arriving at the moment content requires it.

BUT, it is nice to be prepared! If you want to manage improvisation, it probably helps to understand some music theory. It won't hurt. Does anyone disagree with that? Do you think music theory hurt you? No? Okay. You do not wish you could undo it? Is it locking you into a box, maybe a well-tempered Western-music box? Does anyone have a beef with that? Maybe you wish you had learned another tradition. I think sometimes you need names for things, even if what really matters is understanding how they work. But names do help. You hear something and say, that is B, or that is C Lydian, or that is some strange alien mode.

A writer friend of mine once said something similar about reading, though she put it much more dramatically. She was talking directly to my daughter before she could read and telling her not to do it, not to give in to the paternity of language. It was performative, obviously, but it landed. The point was that once you start reading, you are never really outside that system again. A year later, after my daughter learned to read well, she suddenly realized that the world was full of ice cream shops. She had had no idea. Reading changed her world.

I also saw a video of Joni Mitchell talking about her chords. She describes them in terms of feeling rather than formal harmonic names: doubt, inquiry, ominousness, possibility, resolution. At one point she more or less says that she does not know the letter names for all of it and identifies the chords more by how they feel. I like that a lot. It is a good example of a very real musical intelligence that does not line up neatly with formal theory. If you are going to play with Joni Mitchell, you are going to have to learn her chords, whether or not you can map them back onto conventional names. That seems especially apt in her case, since her writing is so bound up with alternate tunings and harmonic structures that do not sit comfortably inside standard guitar pedagogy.

You know, back to a pure, uncomplicated emotion, uncomplicated. Joyous. Positive happy. Well, that's pretty much a minor with an inversion, but this one's.... I don't know the name. Maybe that's a saucer. I don't know the letter or alphabet names of all this stuff. I identify it more by how the chord feels."*

Welcome back. I made a deck for today because our next hurdle is to understand what components make what sounds. If you are going to make a pedal, you are dealing with some technology. It is not the most complex stuff in the world, but it is complex enough, and a little magical. I want you to have enough mastery to figure out when a circuit will block high frequencies, when it will block low frequencies, and how those decisions affect what you hear.

Signal flow is the path taken by a relatively small electrical or digital version of the sound you want. You shape that signal, maybe run it through a low-pass filter, then a high-pass filter, then a distortion stage, and only at the end do you amplify it to speaker level. Passive components are useful because they do not need their own power supply to shape a signal. Resistors and capacitors can filter, attenuate, and redirect energy even though they do not create or amplify it. That can be confusing, but it helps to think in terms of scales: a swing can keep moving once it is going, but a small external push can still change what it is doing.

We have already seen that you can make sound by putting together little modules, high-pass filters, low-pass filters, and different kinds of distortion that clip the ends of waveforms or fold them back in. We have seen all these different components shape sound in different ways, and if you string them together, you get a distortion pedal. That is where we are going. But there are a lot of abstractions there, so I want to make sure we actually know them.

The slide deck and a worksheet that are meant to help with that. The point is to move toward understanding what this stuff is. What are schematics? What are components? How do they relate to one another? How could you compose them on a breadboard to make sound? That is where we are heading. From today forward, you are going to be interacting with these things.

![Breadboard](Breadboard.png)

This is the breadboard. Why is it called that? I do not know. Does it look like bread? Not really. But you are going to be doing something called breadboarding, and one of the first confusions is understanding how this thing connects internally. It is not obvious just by looking at it. The basic idea is that there are these little holes, and behind them there is a hidden pattern of metal connections. Once you see that pattern, it becomes much easier to understand. You are going to be making circuits by putting components into holes on the top side, but what matters is how those holes are connected underneath. That is the real medium you are working in.

This breadboard has a little power board on top of it. It's a handy addition. It can take different kinds of input power, including a battery or USB, and regulate it down to either 5 volts or 3.3 volts, depending on how you set the jumpers. It plugs right into the breadboard rails. I found these pretty cheaply and they are useful because they take care of power for you in a compact way.

So I can take a nine-volt battery, plug it into this board, turn on the switch, and if the light comes on, I know power is coming in. This light is a decent indicator. If the battery is too weak, the light does not come on. That has been my experience so far. Once you have that, you can start looking at the actual circuit. Up on the board you have passive components like capacitors and resistors, and at the bottom there is an IC, an integrated circuit. Those little spider-looking chips are designed so that their legs fit right into the breadboard, which is nice.

There is always this awkward gap between the physical board, which is pretty easy to understand once it is in your hand, and the schematic, which is more like a recipe or sheet music. Neither metaphor is perfect, but both are useful.

In earlier versions of this class, we did more poking things into the board and seeing what happened. That was good in its own way. You could get to sound through experimentation. But I am trying to be a little more thoughtful this time and give you some design background so you can put circuits together more deliberately.

A schematic really involves two different things: power and signal. We talked a lot about signal, and we talked about power, and generally we separate those concepts. The breadboard has power on the outside rails. You need power to run an amplifier chip or a logic chip or anything active, but signals also have energy in them. Still, when you are designing, it helps to think about power and signal separately. We are dealing with direct current for the power supply, which is not supposed to have a waveform. It is just power. Usually we think of positive and negative, and we tend to lump negative together with ground. Negative becomes zero, becomes ground, becomes earth.

That is where the metaphor comes from. When you build a house, you literally pound a metal rod into the ground to "ground" the electrical system. It gives you a reference point against which you can measure everything (and it draws lightning away from the house!). Oftentimes a circuit is more complicated than that. You might have plus nine volts and minus nine volts. But in the simpler cases we are dealing with, ground is just the baseline. If you think of a waveform oscillating, it is oscillating around that zero line. That is the midpoint, like the midpoint of a swing. It goes positive, then negative, back and forth around the center.

So signal flow is a tiny model of the sound you want. You will eventually amplify it so it becomes audible, but first you are shaping signal. You might run it through a low-pass filter, then a high-pass filter, then create some distortion, then amplify the result. Passive components are useful because they do not need external power to shape a signal. Capacitors and resistors can do work on a signal even though they do not create power themselves. That can be confusing, but it helps to think in terms of different scales. A swing can keep moving on its own, but an external push changes what it is doing.

The three main schematic components we are focusing on are the resistor, capacitor, and inductor. The resistor resists current flow. In European schematics it is often drawn as a box instead of the zigzag line, but it is the same thing. The capacitor is drawn as two plates with a gap between them, indicating that charge builds up between conducting surfaces separated by a dielectric. Signal comes in, charge accumulates, and then it releases. It is like a little bucket, or maybe more like static electricity building up and discharging. The inductor is a coil of wire. If you run current through it, it creates magnetism. We are not going to use inductors much yet, but guitar pickups are inductors, so they are coming later.

Today, though, resistors and capacitors are the main focus. A resistor simply resists flow. It is like crimping a hose. You are not necessarily stopping the flow entirely, just restricting it. The unit is the ohm, with the omega symbol. Usually you do not see one-ohm resistors very much in this context. You see one kilo-ohm, twenty-two kilo-ohms, one mega-ohm, values like that. If you do not have exactly the value you want, you can add resistors in series to get there. Two eleven-kilo-ohm resistors in series will get you twenty-two kilo-ohms, for example.

Capacitors are trickier because they care about frequency. Their impedance changes as the frequency changes. Higher frequencies pass through a capacitor more easily, while lower frequencies are resisted more. That makes capacitors incredibly useful for audio. If you want a low-pass filter, you can use a capacitor to draw high frequencies away to ground. If you want a high-pass filter, you rearrange the same basic parts and get the opposite result. Capacitor values are measured in farads, but in practice we work with tiny fractions like microfarads, nanofarads, and picofarads.

Some capacitors are polarized. The little blob-shaped ceramic ones are usually not polarized. The can-shaped electrolytic ones are polarized, which means orientation matters. They have different internal construction, and you have to put them in the circuit the right way around. On schematics you will also see ground symbols, junctions where wires connect, and little bridge symbols showing when wires cross without connecting. That distinction matters. You will also see potentiometers, which are variable resistors. A potentiometer has a wiper that moves along resistive material, changing the effective resistance. It can also act as a voltage divider, which is one of the reasons it is so useful.

If you imagine drawing a heavy graphite line with a pencil, that line can conduct electricity a little bit, but not perfectly. A potentiometer works on a similar principle. The farther the current has to travel through the resistive material, the more resistance it encounters. Move the wiper and you change the amount of resistance. There are variations on this idea too, like photoresistors, which change resistance depending on light. Those are fun to use in audio circuits because they let you control sound with light, almost like a theremin effect.

So once you understand that capacitors care about frequency, passive filters become easier to grasp. Put a capacitor in the right place and you can either let low frequencies pass or let high frequencies pass. A low-pass filter sends the high frequencies to ground and lets the low ones continue. A high-pass filter does the opposite. The exact point where the filter starts cutting frequencies is determined by the values of the resistor and capacitor. There is math for that, and there are calculators online that will do it for you. For now, the important thing is to understand the structure.

Okay, so let me get back into this and talk about why I was discussing how the capacitor kind of draws things away from the circuit.

It is easy to think of the tiny signal in a passive circuit as something like the latent image on a film negative. In both cases, what is there is already real and already articulated, but too faint to perceive directly. It needs some later process to make it available to the senses. We are pretending, for the moment, that amplification is neutral. It is not, really, but that is not the point. The point is that quiet does not mean vague, and invisibility does not mean empty. The small signal already has structure.

There are better analogies for filtering itself. Take a record groove. It is tiny, barely perceivable, but it carries a real physical trace. The stylus follows that groove, and the cartridge turns its movement into a small electrical signal. That signal is real, structured, and meaningful, but too weak to hear directly. From there, later stages shape and amplify it. This is not exactly an analogy for the passive filter alone, but it is a very good analog model for signal flow more generally.

If you want to understand a low-pass filter, imagine touching the stylus lightly with a Q-tip while it rides the groove. The stylus can still move, but not in the same way. The cotton gives some of that motion somewhere else to go. Slow, broader movement can still carry forward, but smaller, quicker jiggles get absorbed much more than slow sways as the cotton compresses and yields. In that sense, the Q-tip is like a capacitor tied to ground at the output point. It does not erase the signal, and it does not amplify it. It provides a side path that preferentially swallows the faster motion, so more of the low-frequency movement remains in the main path while more of the high-frequency motion gets dumped away. That is basically the logic of a simple low-pass filter.

What I like about this analogy is that it stays in the analog world. It lets you think of the signal not as an abstract code but as real movement: a structured trace becoming a structured vibration, then a structured voltage, then finally a sound large enough to hear. The passive circuit does not invent that articulation. It shapes what survives.

When I showed white noise going through a low-pass filter, the idea was that white noise contains all frequencies, more or less evenly distributed. If you run that through the filter and move the cutoff point, you can hear the higher frequencies disappear while the lows remain. It is not a hard cutoff. It is a slope. It rolls off gradually. That slope can be made steeper with more complex filters, but this simple first-order passive filter has a gentle rolloff. The same idea applies when you run musical material through it. If you put a potentiometer in the circuit, you can turn the cutoff point with a knob. That is basically what the tone knob on a guitar is: a low-pass filter with a variable resistor and a capacitor to ground.

Then, if you swap the resistor and capacitor, you get a high-pass filter. Same parts, different arrangement, very different result. It is elegant. Once you start seeing that, the whole thing becomes less magical and more legible. There are some good tools online that help with this. Circuit Canvas lets you drag components around and wire them up visually. EveryCircuit goes further and lets you inspect voltage and current in a very dynamic way. Falstad is another simulator that is especially good for visualizing signal flow and changing component values on the fly. I am new to some of these tools too, but they are really useful for building intuition.

As we worked through the simulations, a lot of the questions that came up were good ones. One student asked how sound, as we understand it in terms of spectra and frequencies, gets translated into electrical behavior in a circuit. That is exactly the right question. The best way to think about it is that the audio signal is a tiny oscillating voltage, a little analog model of the sound. It is not sound yet in the sense of air moving against your ear. It is an electrical pattern that corresponds to the sound. The circuit shapes that pattern, filtering some parts, distorting others, and then later an amplifier and speaker turn it back into sound.

That still feels like magic to a lot of people, and honestly that is fine. A resistor is easy enough to understand: it impedes everything equally. But the capacitor is the strange one, because it responds differently depending on frequency. One way of putting it is that faster oscillations keep charging and discharging it in a way that allows them to pass, while slower ones do not. It is not a perfect metaphor, but it helps. And yes, ground is where some of that energy goes. In the filter model, you are literally sending parts of the signal off to ground.
