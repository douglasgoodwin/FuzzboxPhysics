## 1. Quick frame (3–5 minutes)

On the board, write three categories:

- Air microphone  
- Contact / solid‑borne microphone  
- “Other” transduction (water, EM field, radio, etc.)

Very briefly define:

- Air mic: transduces **pressure waves in air** into electrical signal.  
- Contact mic: “senses audio vibrations through contact with solid objects,” almost insensitive to air, so it transduces **structure‑borne vibration** instead.[1][2][3]
- Electrical Walks / Scanner: coils or radio receivers convert **electromagnetic or radio signals** into audio.[4][5][6][7][8][9][10][11]

Tell them: “For each excerpt, your job is to reverse‑engineer the source: what’s vibrating, in what medium, and what device is deciding what counts as signal?”

***

## 2. Focused excerpts and questions (20–25 minutes)

Play short excerpts (1–2 minutes each) from 4–5 of the pieces you listed. After each one, give them 2 minutes to answer the same three questions on paper.

Suggested sequence:

1. **Toshiya Tsunoda – vibration from a water suction pump (contact on solid)**  
   - Context: contact mic on surfaces to reveal vibrations from a pump and resonances of material.[12][13][14]
   - Students write:  
     1. “What do you think is physically vibrating?” (pipe wall, pump housing, floor, etc.)  
     2. “Through what material is the vibration reaching the mic?” (metal, concrete, water in pipe)  
     3. “Name one sound that you’d expect from an air mic in that room that you do NOT hear here.”

2. **Hydrophone recording (water as medium)**  
   - Context: hydrophones convert pressure changes in water into signal; they reveal underwater events and suppress airborne sounds.[3][1]
   - Students write:  
     1. “Describe the environment you imagine (depth, size, activity) purely from sound.”  
     2. “What physical events are likely happening above the water that are inaudible?”  
     3. “How does water as a medium change what counts as ‘foreground’ sound?”

3. **Jez riley French – architectural / structural contact recording**  
   - Context: uses contact mics on buildings, bridges, glass to reveal hidden vibrations and material resonances.[15][16][17]
   - Students write:  
     1. “Which part of a building or structure do you think the mic is physically touching?”  
     2. “What clues in the sound led you to that guess (steady tones, creaks, impacts, low rumbles)?”  
     3. “What everyday sounds from the same location are missing?”

4. **Christina Kubisch – Electrical Walks (EM field recording)**  
   - Context: custom headphones use built‑in coils that pick up electromagnetic fields from devices and lines, making them audible.[6][9][18][19][20][21][4]
   - Students write:  
     1. “What kinds of human‑made systems do you think are producing these signals (ticket machines, tram lines, LED signs, cables)?”  
     2. “If you stood in the same place with your ears only, what would you hear instead?”  
     3. “What is the ‘space’ of this recording: a room, a city, or a network?”

5. **Scanner – intercepted radio/phone conversations**  
   - Context: early Scanner used radio scanner devices to intercept unencrypted mobile/phone calls and fold them into compositions.[7][8][10][11]
   - Students write:  
     1. “Where are these voices physically located in relation to the ‘microphone’?”  
     2. “What is actually vibrating at the point of capture (antenna, radio circuitry, speaker cone)?”  
     3. “What aspects of the city or social space become audible purely because of this transduction method?”

If time is tight, do 3–4 examples instead of 5, but always keep the same three written questions per excerpt.

***

## 3. Fast compare: air vs non‑air (5–7 minutes)

To anchor their thinking, play a very short **“normal” air‑mic field recording** of a similar environment to one of the above (e.g., a street, building interior, or waterfront).

On a single sheet, have them draw a quick two‑column list:

- Left: “Things the air mic hears that the other methods do not.”  
- Right: “Things the other methods hear that the air mic does not.”

Then ask 2–3 students to share one line from each column out loud.

Close by asking them to write one sentence:

- “In my own fuzzbox project, I now think the sound really comes from ______, and my chosen transducer decides that ______ counts as signal.”
